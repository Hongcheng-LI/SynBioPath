
### ABSTRACT
Halogenated anthraquinones (AQs) exhibit remarkable pharmacological properties; however, the specific halogenases responsible for their enzymatic biosynthesis remain largely unexplored. Through an Enzyme Function Initiative-Enzyme Similarity Tool (EFI-EST) analysis of fungal genomes, we identified a cryptic biosynthetic gene cluster (*cem* BGC) from the deep-sea-derived fungus *Penicillium javense* OUCF108 that uniquely harbors a putative flavin-dependent halogenase (*cemE*). Heterologous expression of the core polyketide synthase and releasing enzymes in *Aspergillus oryzae* NSAR1 led to the isolation of early-stage intermediates (**1-6**). However, the subsequent introduction of tailoring enzymes failed to yield new metabolites. Comparative genomic analysis revealed that the absence of a critical decarboxylase was responsible for the pathway truncation. By mining a surrogate decarboxylase (CemP) from *Talaromyces veerkampii* LYM01, we successfully refactored the silent *cem* BGC, enabling the *de novo* biosynthesis of the key intermediate emodin (**3**). Furthermore, we functionally characterized the novel AQ halogenase CemE via whole-cell biotransformation, confirming its ability to generate 7-chloroemodin (**7**). To enhance its relatively low catalytic efficiency, structure-guided rational engineering using AlphaFold3 and Saport tools identified key peripheral residues. Alanine scanning yielded CemE variants (T498A, T501A, and F543A) with up to a 3-fold increase in catalytic efficiency. Finally, biological evaluations revealed that these halogenated AQs exhibited low cytotoxicity against human A549 cells, highlighting their favorable safety profiles. This study highlights rational pathway refactoring for awakening silent BGCs and provides an engineered halogenase for the biocatalytic synthesis of halogenated aromatic scaffolds.

### IMPORTANCE
Anthraquinones (AQs) are a prominent class of natural products with diverse bioactivities, which can be significantly enhanced by regioselective halogenation. Despite the existence of naturally occurring halogenated AQs, dedicated AQ halogenases for biocatalysis remain exceedingly rare. In this study, we successfully awakened the cryptic *cem* biosynthetic gene cluster by diagnosing and repairing an evolutionary defect (the loss of a decarboxylase) through rational pathway refactoring. Importantly, we discovered and functionally characterized CemE, the first experimentally verified halogenase capable of directly modifying the AQ core. By utilizing AlphaFold3 and the Saport tool, we rationally engineered CemE to significantly improve its catalytic efficiency. These findings not only expand the repertoire of flavin-dependent halogenases but also establish a robust synthetic biology platform for the discovery and biocatalytic optimization of halogenated aromatic therapeutics.

### INTRODUCTION
Halogenated compounds play a pivotal role in pharmaceutical and agricultural fields, contributing significantly to enhanced bioactivity, target binding affinity, and metabolic stability. Consequently, the efficient production of halogenated compounds has attracted tremendous attention. Compared to traditional chemical halogenation, which often requires harsh conditions and lacks regioselectivity, enzymatic halogenation provides an environmentally friendly and highly regio- and stereoselective alternative, making biocatalysis highly valuable for generating diverse halogenated scaffolds.

Among nature's halogenating arsenal, flavin-dependent halogenases (FDHs) are particularly noteworthy. FDHs catalyze the selective electrophilic halogenation of electron-rich aromatic rings and conjugated systems by utilizing FADH₂ and molecular oxygen to generate a highly reactive hypohalous acid intermediate. They functionalize C–H bonds by installing halogen atoms (typically Cl⁻ or Br⁻), generating a diverse array of bioactive halogenated natural products. Due to their strict regioselectivity and mild reaction conditions, FDHs have emerged as highly attractive biocatalysts for industrial applications and synthetic biology.

Anthraquinones (AQs) are a prominent class of polyketide natural products featuring a diverse array of structural variations based on the core emodin scaffold. Widely distributed in plants, fungi, and bacteria, AQs exhibit a broad spectrum of pharmacological activities, including anticancer, antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, and antiviral properties. Noticeably, previous structure-activity relationship studies have revealed that halogenation at specific positions (e.g., C-4, C-5, or C-7) of the AQ core can significantly enhance their antibacterial and antitumor activities. However, despite the discovery of naturally occurring halogenated AQs, the specific halogenases responsible for their enzymatic biosynthesis remain largely unexplored, underscoring the need to identify and characterize dedicated AQ halogenases.

Fungal genomes harbor a vast number of biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) encoding secondary metabolites; however, the majority of these BGCs remain silent or cryptic under standard laboratory conditions. Rational pathway refactoring has proven to be an effective strategy for awakening these silent clusters. In our continuous search for novel halogenating enzymes, we performed a sequence similarity network analysis using the Enzyme Function Initiative-Enzyme Similarity Tool (EFI-EST) to profile reported AQ-associated BGCs. Intriguingly, we identified an uncharacterized BGC (*cem* BGC) from the deep-sea cold-seep derived fungus *Penicillium javense* OUCF108. Unlike most known AQ BGCs, the *cem* BGC uniquely harbors a putative flavin-dependent halogenase gene, *cemE*. We hypothesized that this cluster could be responsible for the biosynthesis of a novel halogenated anthraquinone.

To test this hypothesis, we initially attempted to reconstitute the *cem* BGC in the heterologous host *Aspergillus oryzae* NSAR1. Co-expression of the core polyketide synthase (CemA) and reductase (CemB) successfully yielded early-stage intermediates (**1-6**). However, the subsequent introduction of tailoring enzymes failed to produce new metabolites, indicating a stalled biosynthetic pathway. Through comparative genomic analysis, we identified the absence of a crucial decarboxylase gene as the fundamental cause of the cluster's silence. Herein, we successfully restored the entire biosynthetic pathway by genome mining and introducing a surrogate decarboxylase (CemP), which enabled the *de novo* biosynthesis of emodin (**3**). Furthermore, we functionally characterized the novel AQ halogenase CemE via whole-cell biotransformation, confirming its ability to generate 7-chloroemodin (**7**). To overcome its relatively low catalytic efficiency, we performed structure-guided rational engineering using AlphaFold3 and Saport tools, successfully obtaining CemE variants with up to 3-fold increased catalytic efficiency. Finally, biological evaluations revealed that these compounds possess extremely low cytotoxicity (e.g., against A549 cells), highlighting their favorable safety profiles and indicating their potential as promising scaffolds for further pharmacological optimization.

### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

#### Bioinformatic analysis and identification of the cryptic *cem* BGC
To discover novel halogenated anthraquinones (AQs), we performed a systematic sequence similarity network (SSN) analysis of reported AQ-associated BGCs using the Enzyme Function Initiative-Enzyme Similarity Tool (EFI-EST). Intriguingly, a cryptic biosynthetic gene cluster (*cem* BGC) was identified in the genome of the deep-sea cold-seep-derived fungus *Penicillium javense* OUCF108. Annotation of the *cem* BGC revealed a 15-ORF cluster centered around a non-reducing polyketide synthase (NR-PKS), CemA. The putative tailoring enzymes include an enoyl reductase (CemB), a $\beta$-metallo-lactamase (CemC), a tryptophan halogenase (CemE), and an anthrone oxidase (CemI). Notably, comparative genomic analysis revealed that while the core PKS (CemA) and the product-releasing enzyme (CemC) share high homology with those in known AQ BGCs (e.g., geodin and secalonic acid B), the tailoring enzymes—particularly the halogenase CemE—exhibit unusually low sequence identity. This unique genetic architecture strongly suggested that the activation of the *cem* BGC could lead to the production of structurally distinct, halogenated AQ natural products.

#### Heterologous expression and biosynthesis of early-stage intermediates (1-6)
To elucidate the biosynthetic potential of the *cem* BGC, we adopted a sequential refactoring strategy in the well-established heterologous host *Aspergillus oryzae* NSAR1. Initially, we constructed an expression strain (AO-cemAC) harboring the core PKS *cemA* and the releasing enzyme *cemC*. HPLC analysis of the fermentation broth revealed the production of endocrocin (**2**), confirming the functional expression of the core skeleton enzymes.

Subsequently, we systematically introduced the putative tailoring genes (*cemB*, *cemE*, and *cemI*) individually and in various combinations into the endocrocin-producing AO-cemAC strain (generating strains such as AO-cemACBE and AO-cemACBEI). Unexpectedly, extensive HPLC and LC-MS analyses showed identical metabolic profiles across all transformants, with no new metabolites detected beyond endocrocin (**2**) and related early-stage intermediates (**1**, **4-6**). The structural diversity of compounds **1-6** highlighted the flexibility of the core PKS machinery, but the failure of the tailoring enzymes to functionalize endocrocin (**2**) indicated a premature termination of the biosynthetic pathway.

#### Diagnosis of pathway truncation and successful refactoring via genome mining
To decipher the molecular basis of the *cem* BGC’s silence, we re-evaluated its genetic composition by comparing it with homologous BGCs that utilize endocrocin (**2**) as an intermediate (e.g., agnestin A and cryptosporioptide B BGCs). This analysis uncovered a critical genetic defect: the conversion of endocrocin (**2**) to the key intermediate emodin (**3**) universally requires the synergistic action of a decarboxylase and an anthrone oxidase. However, the *cem* BGC entirely lacks a decarboxylase gene, explaining why the pathway stalled at endocrocin (**2**).

To repair this evolutionary defect, we employed a genome mining strategy using the characterized decarboxylase AgnL1 as a probe. We successfully identified and cloned a homologous decarboxylase gene, *cemP* (68.4% identity to AgnL1), from *Talaromyces veerkampii* LYM01. Co-expression of *cemP* along with the core enzymes and the anthrone oxidase *cemI* in the host (strain AO-cemACPI) led to the successful conversion of endocrocin (**2**) into a new major product. MS and NMR analyses unambiguously identified this product as emodin (**3**). This rational pathway refactoring not only awakened the cryptic *cem* BGC but also definitively proved that the loss of a decarboxylase was the root cause of its silence.

#### Functional characterization of the novel halogenase CemE via whole-cell biotransformation
With the core pathway successfully restored to produce emodin (**3**), we proceeded to investigate the functions of the remaining tailoring enzymes. Introduction of the halogenase CemE into the emodin-producing strain (AO-cemACPIE) resulted in the generation of a new halogenated product, which was isolated and structurally elucidated as 7-chloroemodin (**7**) based on HR-ESI-MS and NMR data.

To biochemically characterize CemE, we initially attempted *in vitro* enzymatic assays using purified recombinant CemE. However, regardless of the cofactors used (NADH or NADPH) or the addition of flavin reductases, no conversion of free emodin (**3**) was observed *in vitro*. We reasoned that CemE might require a specific intracellular environment or auxiliary factors absent in the *in vitro* system. Therefore, we employed an *Escherichia coli* whole-cell biotransformation strategy. By feeding emodin (**3**) to *E. coli* BL21(DE3) expressing CemE, we successfully detected the conversion of **3** into 7-chloroemodin (**7**) with an initial conversion rate of approximately 5.15%. Optimizing the induction time to 36 hours significantly increased the conversion efficiency to ~18%, establishing a robust platform for further enzyme engineering. Phylogenetic analysis further revealed that CemE clusters distinctly with phenolic acid-preferring FDHs rather than typical tryptophan halogenases, consistent with its activity towards the phenolic AQ scaffold.

#### Rational engineering of CemE for enhanced catalytic efficiency
Given the relatively low conversion efficiency of wild-type CemE, we sought to engineer the enzyme to enhance its biocatalytic performance. We utilized AlphaFold3 to predict the 3D structure of CemE, which exhibited a typical FDH fold highly similar to the homologous protein RadH (RMSD = 0.918 Å). Subsequent molecular docking of emodin (**3**) and the FAD cofactor into the CemE structure using AutoDock Vina, combined with analysis using the Saport tool, identified key residues within a 5 Å radius of the catalytic center. Emodin (**3**) was predicted to form critical hydrogen bonds with residues S48, F332, and S333, and $\pi$-$\pi$ interactions with F332.

Guided by these structural insights, we performed a systematic alanine scanning mutagenesis on the identified active site residues. As expected, mutating the essential catalytic residue K74, or the key substrate-binding residues S48, F332, and S333 to alanine completely abolished halogenation activity. Remarkably, however, the mutants T498A, T501A, and F543A exhibited a 2- to 3-fold increase in catalytic efficiency compared to the wild-type enzyme. Structural analysis suggests that these residues are located at the periphery of the substrate-binding pocket; substituting them with the smaller alanine likely reduced steric hindrance, thereby broadening the substrate access channel or optimizing the catalytic conformation.

#### Biological evaluations of the halogenated anthraquinones
Halogenation is a proven strategy for enhancing the pharmacological properties of natural products. To explore the therapeutic potential of the generated AQs and their halogenated derivatives, we evaluated their biological activities against various targets. Cytotoxicity assays against the human lung adenocarcinoma cell line A549 revealed that all tested compounds (including emodin **3** and 7-chloroemodin **7**) exhibited extremely low cytotoxicity, with relative cell viabilities remaining near 100% at the tested concentrations. Similarly, *in vitro* antiviral assays against Hepatitis B virus (HBV) and Influenza A virus (IAV) showed mild inhibitory effects without pronounced cellular toxicity. These results demonstrate that the *cem* BGC-derived AQs possess favorable safety profiles. Although their direct inhibitory activities in current screening models are moderate, their low toxicity highlights them as safe and promising molecular scaffolds. Future efforts, such as targeted glycosylation, could be employed to further optimize their solubility and pharmacological efficacy.

### MATERIALS AND METHODS

#### Strains, plasmids, and culture conditions
The deep-sea cold-seep-derived fungus *Penicillium javense* OUCF108 was used as the source of the *cem* biosynthetic gene cluster (BGC). *Talaromyces veerkampii* LYM01 was used for the genome mining of the decarboxylase gene *cemP*. *Escherichia coli* DH5$\alpha$ was used for routine DNA cloning and plasmid construction, while *E. coli* BL21(DE3) was employed for heterologous protein expression and whole-cell biotransformation. *E. coli* strains were cultured in Luria-Bertani (LB) medium at 37°C, supplemented with appropriate antibiotics (e.g., 50 $\mu$g/mL kanamycin or 100 $\mu$g/mL ampicillin) when necessary. *Aspergillus oryzae* NSAR1 was used as the heterologous host for pathway refactoring and was cultured on DPY agar or liquid medium at 28°C. The fungal expression vectors pTAex3, pUSA, pAdeA, and pPTRI, along with the bacterial expression vector pET28a(+), were used for gene assembly and expression.

#### Bioinformatic analysis and structural modeling
To profile anthraquinone (AQ)-associated BGCs, a sequence similarity network (SSN) was generated using the Enzyme Function Initiative-Enzyme Similarity Tool (EFI-EST) and visualized with Cytoscape. The homologous BGCs and their gene functions were analyzed using antiSMASH and BLASTp against the NCBI database. 
For structural analysis of the halogenase CemE, its 3D structure was predicted using the AlphaFold3 algorithm. The FAD cofactor and the substrate emodin (**3**) were docked into the predicted CemE structure using AutoDock Vina. To guide the rational engineering of CemE, the Saport tool was utilized to identify potential mutation hotspots and evaluate the structural dynamics of residues within a 5 Å radius of the catalytic pocket.

#### DNA manipulation and pathway refactoring in *A. oryzae*
Genomic DNA and cDNA were extracted from fungal strains using standard protocols. For the reconstitution of the *cem* BGC, the core PKS gene *cemA* and the releasing enzyme gene *cemC* were cloned into the *A. oryzae* expression vectors. To identify the missing decarboxylase, the genome of *T. veerkampii* LYM01 was mined, and the target gene *cemP* was amplified. The tailoring genes (*cemB*, *cemE*, *cemI*, and *cemP*) were amplified using specific primers and assembled into the fungal expression vectors (pAdeA, pPTRI, pTAex3) under the control of the constitutive *amyB* promoter and *amyB* terminator via homologous recombination. The constructed plasmids were sequentially transformed into *A. oryzae* NSAR1 using the established protoplast-PEG method. Transformants were selected on Czapek-Dox minimal medium lacking the corresponding nutritional markers.

#### Fermentation, extraction, and metabolite analysis
The *A. oryzae* transformants were cultivated in DPY liquid medium at 28°C for 5-7 days with shaking at 200 rpm. The fermentation broth was extracted with an equal volume of ethyl acetate three times. The organic layers were combined, evaporated under reduced pressure, and re-dissolved in methanol for HPLC analysis. 
Analytical HPLC was performed on an Agilent 1260 system equipped with a reverse-phase C18 column, using a gradient of water (containing 0.1% formic acid) and acetonitrile. High-resolution electrospray ionization mass spectra (HR-ESI-MS) were acquired on a Q-TOF mass spectrometer. For structural elucidation of the isolated products (e.g., 7-chloroemodin, **7**), 1D and 2D NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker Avance 400 or 600 MHz spectrometer using DMSO-$d_6$ as the solvent.

#### Protein expression and *in vitro* enzymatic assays
The *cemE* gene was amplified from the cDNA of *P. javense* OUCF108 and cloned into pET28a(+) to generate an N-terminal His$_6$-tagged fusion construct. The plasmid was transformed into *E. coli* BL21(DE3). Protein expression was induced with 0.5 mM IPTG at 16°C overnight. The recombinant CemE protein was purified using Ni-NTA affinity chromatography and analyzed by SDS-PAGE.
For the *in vitro* halogenation assay, the reaction mixture (100 $\mu$L) contained 100 mM Tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.5), 10 $\mu$M purified CemE, 100 $\mu$M emodin (**3**), 1 mM NADH or NADPH, 1 $\mu$M flavin reductase (PrnF), and 50 $\mu$M FAD. The reaction was incubated at 30°C for 4 hours, quenched with methanol, and analyzed by HPLC.

#### Whole-cell biotransformation and site-directed mutagenesis
Due to the lack of *in vitro* activity, a whole-cell biotransformation strategy was employed to characterize CemE and its mutants. *E. coli* BL21(DE3) harboring pET28a-*cemE* was cultured to an OD$_{600}$ of 0.6 and induced with 0.1 mM IPTG. Simultaneously, the substrate emodin (**3**) was added to the culture to a final concentration of 0.5 mM. The biotransformation was carried out optimized at 16°C for 36 hours for maximum conversion. The culture was then extracted with ethyl acetate and analyzed by HPLC to determine the conversion rate.
Guided by the docking and Saport models, site-directed mutagenesis (alanine scanning) was performed using PCR-based methods with appropriate primer pairs. The resulting mutant plasmids (e.g., T498A, T501A, F543A) were verified by DNA sequencing and transformed into *E. coli* BL21(DE3) for whole-cell biocatalytic evaluation under the identical conditions described above.

#### Biological activity assays
To evaluate the therapeutic potential and safety profiles of the generated AQs, cytotoxicity and antiviral assays were performed. 
*   **Cytotoxicity assay:** The viability of human lung adenocarcinoma A549 cells was evaluated. Cells were treated with the tested compounds at designated concentrations. Cell viability was determined using standard colorimetric assays and expressed as a percentage relative to the DMSO control group.
*   **Antiviral assays:** Anti-Hepatitis B virus (HBV) activity was evaluated using an HBX-luminescence-based reporter system. The luminescence intensity was measured to reflect the inhibition of viral replication. Anti-Influenza A virus (IAV) activity was assessed by measuring viral RNA levels relative to the host 18S rRNA internal control using quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) at a designated multiplicity of infection (MOI = 0.1).

### CONCLUSION
In summary, the cryptic *cem* biosynthetic gene cluster from *P. javense* OUCF108 was successfully awakened through rational pathway refactoring in the heterologous host *A. oryzae*. By identifying the missing decarboxylase through comparative genomic analysis and compensating for this evolutionary defect with the mined enzyme CemP, the stalled biosynthetic pathway was reconstructed, enabling the *de novo* biosynthesis of emodin (**3**). Subsequently, we functionally characterized the novel flavin-dependent halogenase CemE via an optimized whole-cell biotransformation strategy, demonstrating its capability to produce 7-chloroemodin (**7**). Furthermore, guided by structural predictions and the Saport tool, site-directed mutagenesis of peripheral active-site residues successfully yielded engineered CemE variants with enhanced catalytic efficiency. Biological evaluations indicated that these halogenated AQs possess excellent safety profiles with low cytotoxicity. Our findings demonstrate the power of combining pathway refactoring, genome mining, and rational enzyme engineering to discover novel biocatalysts and expand the chemical space of halogenated natural products for future pharmacological exploration.
